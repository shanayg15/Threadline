Most DTC brands already pay to text their customers. The problem isn't reach — it's that almost all of that texting is a **broadcast**: the same message, blasted to a segment, optimized for a click in the next ten minutes. It works, a little, and then the channel goes quiet until the next campaign.

A broadcast can't do the one thing that actually drives repeat revenue: have a conversation.

## A list is not a relationship

When you send a campaign, you're talking _at_ a segment. The customer can't usefully reply — there's no one on the other end who knows who they are, what they bought, or why they're asking. So they don't reply. Engagement collapses to a click-through rate, and the most valuable signal a customer can give you — a question, a hesitation, a complaint they'd rather tell you than a return portal — never gets sent.

A persistent thread flips that. There's always someone (or something) on the other end that remembers the whole relationship. The customer can ask "does this run small?" at 11pm and get a real answer grounded in your catalog. That's not a campaign. That's an associate.

## The post-purchase window is wide open

Here's the part brands consistently leave on the table. You spend real money notifying a customer that their package shipped — and then you go silent the moment it lands, which is _exactly_ when the experience is being decided.

The box arrives. It fits, or it doesn't. They love it, or they're quietly drafting a return. A broadcast can't tell the difference, so it does nothing. A thread can show up with a simple, well-timed "how's it working out?" and turn a forming return into an exchange, a question into a reorder, a one-time buyer into a regular.

## What "persistent" actually buys you

- **Context that compounds.** Every message reads the same customer record, so the agent never asks a question it should already know the answer to.
- **A channel that's always warm.** The customer doesn't have to dig up a support email or hunt through a returns portal. They just reply to the thread they're already in.
- **Signal you can't get from a click.** Fit blockers, product confusion, buying intent, reorder timing — in the customer's own words, attached to the order that prompted them.

## Broadcast still has a job

None of this means campaigns are dead. Broadcasts are great at what they're built for: announcing a launch, moving inventory, reaching everyone at once. Threadline doesn't replace that. It fills the gap broadcasts were never designed to cover — the slow, one-to-one, context-rich conversation that happens right before checkout and right after delivery.

One is a megaphone. The other is a thread. You want both — but only one of them brings customers back.
