Here's the most comfortable lie in retention marketing: _we sent a message, then a sale happened, so the message caused the sale._

It's comfortable because it always works. Some fraction of customers were going to buy again anyway. If you message all of them and then take credit for every nearby purchase, your dashboard will look fantastic — and you'll have learned nothing about whether the program actually changed anyone's behavior.

The fix is older than software: a control group.

## Last-touch attribution flatters everything

Last-touch is the default because it's easy. A customer clicks a link, buys within the window, and the channel that owned the last click books the revenue. The trouble is that it counts the buyers who needed no convincing right alongside the ones who did, and it can't tell them apart.

For a post-purchase program this is especially misleading, because you're talking to people who already bought from you once. A meaningful share will reorder on their own. Attribute all of those reorders to your check-in and you've manufactured a number that goes up no matter what you do.

## A holdout makes the question answerable

The honest version is simple to state: **randomly hold back a slice of eligible customers and never proactively message them.** Everyone else is treatment. Then compare.

- Treatment group: gets the check-ins, the exchange nudges, the well-timed reorders.
- Holdout group: identical in every way except they're left alone.

The difference in outcomes between the two _is_ the lift. Not "revenue near a link" — the incremental revenue that exists only because the program exists. It's a smaller, less flattering number than last-touch will ever show you. It's also the only one worth putting in front of a finance team.

## Building it in from the first conversation

A holdout only works if it's there from the start and respected without exception. That means:

- **Stable assignment.** A customer is treatment or holdout once, deterministically — not re-rolled every campaign.
- **Respected at every send.** The holdout is checked when a message is scheduled _and_ again right before it goes out. A customer who slips into "treatment" by accident quietly poisons the comparison.
- **Honest reporting.** Until a real pilot has run, you show counts and assist-based attribution — and you don't imply a lift number you haven't actually measured.

That last point matters more than it sounds. It's tempting to put a big percentage on a landing page. But a made-up lift number on a real product is worse than no number at all: it's the one claim a skeptical operator will test first, and the fastest way to lose them.

Measure it properly, leave the holdout alone, and report what you can actually defend. The number will be smaller — and real.
